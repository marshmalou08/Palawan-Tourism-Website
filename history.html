History of Palawan

A History Written in Caves and Coral

Palawan's human history stretches back at least 47,000 years. The Tabon Caves in Quezon municipality contain some of the oldest human skeletal remains ever found in the Philippines — the Tabon Man — evidence of a prehistoric population that inhabited the island when sea levels were dramatically lower and Palawan was connected to the Borneo landmass.

Long before Spanish colonization, Palawan was a prosperous trading hub connected to the wider maritime Southeast Asian world. Chinese traders visited regularly for swallows' nests, beeswax, and jungle products. By the time the Spanish arrived in the sixteenth century, the northern parts of Palawan were already influenced by Islam from the south.

Spanish rule brought Catholic missions and colonial administration. The construction of Fort Santa Isabel in Taytay in 1667 reflects the Spanish attempt to establish a defensive presence. American colonial rule from 1898 brought roads, schools, and a new administrative framework. Puerto Princesa was developed as the provincial capital. After Philippine independence in 1946, Palawan remained lightly populated — a quality that paradoxically preserved its extraordinary natural heritage.

Historical Highlights

  • The Tabon Caves have yielded human remains dating back approximately 47,000 years.
  • Palawan maintained active trade with China, Borneo, and the Malay world for centuries before Spanish arrival.
  • Fort Santa Isabel in Taytay (1667) is one of the oldest Spanish fortifications in western Palawan.
  • The Puerto Princesa Subterranean River became a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1999.
  • Tubbataha Reef Natural Park became a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1993.
